GREENHOUSE GAS EMISSIONS





Energy is the dominant contributor because the majority of our global energy systems are powered by fossil fuels, which produce substantial emissions. Sectors such as energy use in residential buildings, road transport, and industry are significant sub-categories within the energy sector, highlighting how deeply energy consumption is integrated into daily life and economic activity.



This chart illustrates the relative composition of greenhouse gas emissions from carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O) between 1950 to 2022. CO₂ has consistently been the largest contributor to emissions, accounting for approximately 70% of the total, while methane remains around 20-25%, and nitrous oxide fluctuates between 5-10%. The overall composition has remained relatively stable.

Human greenhouse gas emissions have increased global average temperatures

Early 20th Century (1900–1940): A gradual increase in temperature anomalies is observed, linked to early industrialization and rising greenhouse gas emissions. Warming during this period was modest.

Late 20th Century (1970–2000): A sharp increase in temperature anomalies occurred, reflecting accelerated global warming as CO₂ emissions and industrial activity rose sharply. This period marks the start of modern awareness of climate change.

21st Century (2000–2020): The highest temperature anomalies are seen in the most recent years, with 2020 near the top of the chart. This reflects continued rapid global warming driven by human activities like fossil fuel burning and deforestation.


Global Warming Potential (GWP) is a measure used to compare the ability of different greenhouse gases to trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere. The higher the GWP, the more heat a gas traps in the atmosphere per unit mass compared to CO₂.
